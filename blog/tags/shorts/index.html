The Backwards Law: Why Trying Harder Doesn't Always Work

The Backwards Law: The Art of Letting Go​

The "Backwards Law," a term famously explored by philosopher Alan Watts, posits a counter-intuitive truth: The harder you try to get a result, the further away it moves.

The Paradox of Desire​

Alan Watts was a British writer who translated Eastern philosophy for the Western world. He famously wrote:

"Wanting a positive experience is a negative experience; accepting a negative experience is a positive experience."

This is the core of the Backwards Law. When you pursue happiness, you are reinforcing the fact that you lack it in the first place. This constant chase makes you less satisfied.

Real-Life Examples:​

  1. Sleep: The more you worry about falling asleep, the more awake you stay.
  2. Social Ease: The harder you try to "look cool" or "act natural," the more awkward you become.
  3. Confidence: The truly confident person doesn't need to feel confident; they are simply okay with whatever happens.

Conclusion: Sometimes, stepping back and accepting the current situation—even if it's negative—is the fastest way to improve it. In many situations, struggling only makes the water murkier.
